Service detail

Mowing and edging that makes the whole property read cleaner.

This service is for recurring cuts, one-time lawn resets, and properties where the visual quality depends on precise edges, consistent height, and careful handling around beds, walks, and irrigation.

Service depth

More than a pass with a mower.

Mowing and edging is the most visible recurring service, so it has to account for the property's architecture: where the lawn meets hardscape, how beds are shaped, where equipment can travel, and what finish level the homeowner expects.

What homeowners usually mean

A tidy lawn, sharp borders, no clumps on walkways, no missed corners, and a cut that fits the grass condition rather than a one-height-fits-all approach.

The request should clarify whether this is a maintenance visit, a first cut after overgrowth, or a visual reset before listing, guests, or HOA review.

What providers need to know

Grass height, access, slope, edge length, wet spots, obstacles, pets, and whether clippings should be mulched, controlled, or cleared from hard surfaces.

Photos of the worst area matter more than perfect lawn photos.

What it covers

Mowing, string trimming, sidewalk and driveway edging, bed-line cleanup, light clipping control, and notes for recurring maintenance adjustments.

When users need it

Weekly growth, overgrown edges, rental turnovers, pre-listing cleanup, HOA expectations, or a yard that looks unfinished even after a basic cut.

Details that matter

Preferred cut height, grass condition, slope, wet areas, sprinkler heads, pets, gate width, narrow side yards, and delicate plant beds.

Typical approach

Providers confirm access and scope, cut to an agreed height, trim perimeter zones, sharpen hardscape edges, and flag any turf or drainage issues noticed on site.

Visual read

Clean mowing is seen from the ground up.

A sharp service visit creates continuous visual lines: turf meets stone cleanly, beds are not chewed up, and trimmed areas do not look scalped or uneven.

Good photo angles

Send one wide yard view, one close edge view, one gate/access image, and one photo of the tallest grass or most difficult area.

Scope map

What gets clarified before the visit.

The goal is to avoid vague “just mow it” requests and make sure provider pricing accounts for the details that take real time.

Cut height

Normal maintenance height, first-cut reset height, wet grass risk, and whether the lawn is recently seeded or stressed.

Edge length

Sidewalks, driveway, curb, patios, stepping paths, bed borders, tree rings, fences, and tight side-yard transitions.

Site cautions

Sprinkler heads, exposed roots, toys, pet areas, low lighting, steep grade, soft soil, and fragile planting zones.

Field context

Small conditions change the right mowing plan.

Access

Gate width, parking, locked areas, pets, and equipment path to the backyard.

Texture

Thick growth, crabgrass patches, uneven soil, damp zones, and thin areas that need lighter handling.

Finish

Clippings, hardscape cleanup, bed protection, and how crisp the edge should be for the property style.

Practical questions

Common mowing details.

Can an overgrown lawn be handled as a regular mow?

Sometimes, but tall or wet grass may require a reset cut, extra passes, bagging, or a different estimate.

Should clippings be removed?

That depends on grass height, lawn health, property expectations, and provider equipment. The provider confirms options.

What makes edging take longer?

Long curb runs, neglected bed lines, tight fences, tree rings, patios, and hardscape with heavy overgrowth.
